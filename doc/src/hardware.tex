\subsection{Battery Management System}
\label{sec_bms}
The battery must be protected against deep-discharge, over-charge, overcurrent and overtemperature. (\ReqRef{R:BMS})
This is implemented with the battery monitor and protector BQ76905. It is powered directly from the batteries and can be configured through its \ac{I2C} interface. The cells are connected to the battery monitor with a \qty{100}{\ohm} resistor for overcurrent protection. These resistors also limit the balancing current. 
\begin{align}
    I_{Bal} 
    = \dfrac{U_{Bal}}{R_{Bal}} 
    = \dfrac{n_{Bal} \cdot U_{cell}}{2 \cdot R_C + n_{Bal} \cdot R_{CB}}
    = \dfrac{n_{Bal} \cdot U_{cell}}{2 \cdot \qty{100}{\ohm} + n_{Bal} \cdot R_{CB}}
\end{align}
\begin{align}
    I_{Bal_{typ}} 
    = \dfrac{n_{Bal_{typ}} \cdot U_{cell_{typ}}}{2 \cdot R_C + n_{Bal_{typ}} \cdot R_{CB_{typ}}}
    = \dfrac{1 \cdot \qty{3.65}{\volt}}{2 \cdot \qty{100}{\ohm} + 1 \cdot \qty{93}{\ohm}}
    = \qty{12.46}{\milli\ampere}
\end{align}
\begin{align}
    I_{Bal_{min}} 
    = \dfrac{n_{Bal_{min}} \cdot U_{cell_{min}}}{2 \cdot R_C + n_{Bal_{min}} \cdot R_{CB_{max}}}
    = \dfrac{1 \cdot \qty{2.0}{\volt}}{2 \cdot \qty{100}{\ohm} + 1 \cdot \qty{200}{\ohm}}
    = \qty{5}{\milli\ampere}
\end{align}

\begin{align}
    I_{Bal_{max}} 
    = \dfrac{n_{Bal_{max}} \cdot U_{cell_{max}}}{2 \cdot R_C + n_{Bal_{max}} \cdot R_{CB_{min}}}
    = \dfrac{4 \cdot \qty{3.65}{\volt}}{2 \cdot \qty{100}{\ohm} + 4 \cdot \qty{53}{\ohm}}
    = \qty{35.44}{\milli\ampere}
\end{align}
All signals directly connected to the battery cells are assigned to the netclass BAT to enforce a minimum clearance. A capacitor with a value of \qty{100}{\nano\farad} is connected to each cell voltage measurement for filtering as recommended by the datasheet \cite{TI:BQ76905}. \autoref{fig_bms_volt} shows the cell connection to the \ac{BMS}.

The temperature of the battery is measured with a \qty{10}{\kilo\ohm} \ac{NTC} thermistor with a B value of \qty{3380}{\kelvin}. The \ac{BMS} includes a pull-up resistor with a value of \qty{20}{\kilo\ohm}, therefore no external pull-up resistor is necessary. The temperature sensor is soldered to the bottom side of the PCB and glued to the cells using thermally conductive adhesive. A \qty{1}{\nano\farad} capacitor is connected in parallel to the thermistor for filtering. For testing purpose, an \ac{SMD} thermistor is placed on the PCB as well, which can be activated by bridging the solder-jumper J1. The temperature sensor is shown in \autoref{fig_bms_temp}.

The \ac{BMS} can be woken up from deepsleep or shutdown mode by a rising edge on the pins TS or VC0. In order to allow the microcontroller to wake up the \ac{BMS}, a \ac{GPIO} pin is connected to the TS pin through a protection circuit (\autoref{fig_bms_wakeup}), which keeps the voltage on the TS pin within the absolute maximum ratings of \qty{2.1}{\volt}. \cite{TI:BQ76905}

The battery current is measured with a \qty{5}{\milli\ohm} shunt resistor. The signal is filtered (2x \qty{100}{\ohm}, \qty{100}{\nano\farad}) and connected to the battery monitor. The voltage across the shunt resistor is also amplified by a difference amplifier and connected to the \ac{ADC} of the microcontroller for current control during battery charging.

The CHG and DSG signals connected to the on/off controller instead of directly controlling \acp{MOSFET}. As the low side is not switched, no \ac{I2C} isolator or level shifter is needed. 
The \ac{BMS} is supplied from the batteries through the pins BAT and REGSRC. Both pins are bypassed with a \qty{1}{\micro\farad} capacitor and individually connected to the top of the stack (\verb!VBAT!) through a shottky diode and a \qty{10}{\ohm} series resistor. The output of the internal voltage regulator (REGOUT) is made available for powering standby operations like buttons for powering on or an \ac{RTC} with a voltage of \qty{3.3}{\volt}. A standby battery voltage (\verb!VBAT_STBY!) is provided for the on/off controller. This voltage is available as long as all battery parameters are within the allowed range.

\subsection{On/off controller}
\label{sec_onoff}
The \ac{MOSFET} Q6 blocks any charging current to the battery when the \ac{BMS} does not allow charging. Q5 prevents discharge of the batteries and is the main power switch for the device. A precharge circuit is implemented parallel to Q5 to limit the inrush current. The precharge current is set to \qty{100}{\milli\ampere}. The precharge current runs through the resistors R22 $\ldots$ R25. The \ac{BJT} Q4 pulls the gate of Q5 up when the voltage across the resistors reaches the base-emiter voltage of aproximately \qty{0.65}{\volt}. \autoref{fig_onoff_switch} shows the mains switch \acp{MOSFET} and the precharge circuit. 
\begin{align}
    I_{precharge} = \dfrac{U_{BE} \cdot n_{R_{Precharge}}}{R_{Precharge}}
    = \dfrac{\qty{0.65}{\volt} \cdot 4}{\qty{27}{\ohm}} = \qty{96.3}{\milli\ampere}
\end{align}

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[page=3, scale=\schscale, trim=420 560 230 50, clip]{\schfilemain}
    \caption{On/off controller - highside switch with precharge circuit}
    \label{fig_onoff_switch}
\end{figure}

The charging \ac{MOSFET} Q6 is directly controlled by the \ac{BMS} through the signal \verb!BMS_ON_OFF.CHG!, which is level-shifted and inverted with Q17. The discharging \ac{MOSFET} Q5 can be enabled by the signal \verb!UI_ON_OFF.ON! (Q14), which is active when any of the on-enabled buttons (see \ref{sec_ui_button} \nameref{sec_ui_button}) is pressed. The microcontroller is able to keep the device running by the signal \verb!ON_REQ! (Q15) which should be set high immediatelly after boot. To turn the device off, the microcontroller sets the signal \verb!ON_REQ! low. The on logic is only activated through Q16, when the signal \verb!BMS_ON_OFF.DSG! is active. After enabling the device, the precharge circuit is activated through R40. The gate voltage of the precharge \ac{MOSFET} Q3 is monitored by Q11. When precharge is completed, the current limiter is inactive, which causes the gate voltage to drop to 0, which enables the main \ac{MOSFET} Q5 through the level-shifter Q11 and Q10. 

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[page=3, scale=\schscale, trim=570 80 166 190, clip]{\schfilemain}
    \caption{On/off controller - logic}
    \label{fig_onoff_logic}
\end{figure}

\FloatBarrier

To switch the device off when the microcontroller is unable to turn off the device, an emergency shut-off is implemented as shown in \autoref{fig_onoff_emergency_off}. Pressing the ESC button pulls the signal BUTTON\_OFF high. This charges the capacitor C17 through the level-shifter Q13, Q9. The comparator U3 compares the voltage across the capacitor with half of the supply voltage and disables the \ac{MOSFET} Q5 and the precharge Q3 through the level shifters Q12, Q7 and Q8. The comparator is primarilly supplied from the output of the on/off controller VIN through the diode D5. To keep the \ac{MOSFET} disabled until the ESC button is released, the comparator is also supplied with the BUTTON\_OFF signal through D7. Without that secondary supply, the ESC button would turn on the device when the supply of the comparator is discharged.

\subsubsection{Pinout and module usage}
\paragraph{Reference voltage}
The internal reference voltage is only directly accessible in the BGA package. To access the internal voltage reference, the output of the operational amplifier (OPAMP2\_VOUT) is used. For this the amplifiert must be configured to output the refernce voltage. (OPAMP2\_CSR: TSTREF) the referance voltage is then accessible on PA6. 

\subsubsection{Memory}
To store persistent data such as serial number or calibration data, an \ac{I2C} \ac{EEPROM} is used.

\subsubsection{Clearance}
\label{sec_layout_cleraance}
All signals directly connected to the battery are assignet to the netclass BAT to enforce a minumum clearance of \qty{0.5}{\milli\meter}. An exception is implemented for the gate of Q5 and I2C.SCL. This is necessary due to the low clearance of the packages of the \ac{MOSFET} Q5 and the \ac{BMS} U1.
